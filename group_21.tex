\section{1. Introduction}\label{introduction}

Data analytics has become an increasingly integral part of a company's
decision making process. Utilizing data to uncover patterns, identify
opportunities, and monitor business performance are practices synonymous
with modern corporations. This document shall cover the various
statistical and modeling topics covered in preparation for the Business
Analytics course, and shall relate these to real-world applications.

\subsection{Business Context}\label{business-context}

The modern world has become progressively digitized, with this the share
of online/eCommerce marketplaces have garnered an ever increasing
percentage of the global retail sales market share. As of the writing of
this document the percentage lies around 22.2\% (Buck, 2026). With
growth consistently higher than retail, one of the major countries of
growth is Brazil. Attaining a growth rate of 15.4\% it was the 5th
largest growth country in 2023 (Oberlo, 2024).

One of the major eCommerce platforms in Brazil is Olist. Like many
eCommerce platforms, Olist suffers from delayed deliveries (Across the
dataset, 6.6\% of delivered orders arrived after the estimated delivery
date.), which inevitably leads to poor customer reviews, platform churn,
and high customer support costs. Because Olist does not own the
inventory or directly control the dispatch process, the root cause of
these delays often stems from individual seller behavior (e.g., poor
logistics management, slow processing times).

\subsection{Problem Statement}\label{problem-statement}

Currently, Olist's approach to delayed deliveries is purely reactive,
dealing with angry customers only \emph{after} the estimated delivery
date has already been missed. This makes the process inherently
suboptimal, with delays leaving customers unsattisfied. While eCommerce
platforms are largely platforms for outside sellers to place, market,
and sell their products, in the case of an unfavorable experience it is
still Olist which suffers reputational damage.

\subsection{Project Goal}\label{project-goal}

With the context laid out Problem Statement, the goal of this project is
therefore to reduce support costs and protect the platform's brand
reputation. More specifically this shall be done by identifying where
Olist needs to implement a proactive mechanism to identify at-risk
orders before they fail, as well as a strategic framework to manage
underperforming sellers.

\section{2. Data Analysis
Giannis/Jonas}\label{data-analysis-giannisjonas}

Having covered the problem and goal of the project, as well as having
obtained the Olist eCommerce data, focus may now be shifted towards the
analyzing, cleaning, and transforming of the data. The first of which
will be the analysis of what all is included in the given data, and to
see what may be of use in further modelling down the line.

The analysis shall be done by adopting a two-stage analytical approach
to transition Olist from reactive to proactive logistics management.
Attempting to predict and in turn prevent delays before they occur, This
shall be done as such:

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{Unsupervised Learning (Strategic Seller Segmentation):} First,
  the features will be engineered to capture historical logistics
  performance and pricing behavior at the \emph{seller level} (e.g.,
  average delay rate, average freight cost, dispatch speed). Then the
  \textbf{K-Means Clustering} shall be used to segment the sellers into
  distinct performance tiers (e.g., ``High-Reliability,'' ``Slow \&
  Cheap,'' ``High-Risk'').
\item
  \textbf{Supervised Learning (Tactical Order Forecasting):} Second,
  these sellers segments will be mapped back to the individual order
  level. These segments will be used as a key predictor in a
  classification model (such as a Random Forest or decision tree) to
  forecast whether a \emph{future} individual order is likely to be
  delivered late.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Business Impact}

This pipeline enables a dual-pronged business strategy. Strategically,
Olist can use the seller clusters to penalize or coach ``High-Risk''
sellers (addressing the root cause). Tactically, the predictive model
allows Olist's customer service team to identify high-risk orders the
moment they are placed, enabling proactive communication with the
customer or the deployment of expedited shipping interventions
(addressing the symptoms).

\subsection{Seller-Level Feature
Engineering}\label{seller-level-feature-engineering}

The first stage of our analytical pipeline requires building a
performance profile for each seller. Rather than analysing individual
orders in isolation, we aggregate historical order data to the seller
level, capturing each seller's logistics behaviour across all their past
orders. These features will serve as the input to our K-Means clustering
model.

We engineer four features that capture distinct dimensions of seller
performance:

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{Late rate:} the proportion of a seller's orders delivered
  after the estimated date --- our primary measure of reliability
\item
  \textbf{Avg dispatch days:} the average number of days between order
  purchase and carrier handoff --- captures how quickly a seller
  processes and ships orders
\item
  \textbf{Avg freight cost:} the average shipping cost charged across a
  seller's orders --- a proxy for logistics complexity and geographic
  reach
\item
  \textbf{Order volume:} the total number of fulfilled orders ---
  distinguishes established sellers from low-activity ones whose metrics
  may be unreliable
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Scaling for Clustering}\label{scaling-for-clustering}

scale of input features. A seller with an average freight cost of R\$50

proportion between 0 and 1, simply due to the difference in units rather
than any meaningful difference in importance. We therefore standardise
all four features to zero mean and unit variance before clustering.
